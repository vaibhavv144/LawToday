Constitutional Law
Constitution of India
📅 In force: Jan 26, 1950 📖 395 Articles 🔖 22 Parts
AI-assisted · Expert-reviewed before publication
"WE, THE PEOPLE OF INDIA, having solemnly resolved to constitute India into a SOVEREIGN SOCIALIST SECULAR DEMOCRATIC REPUBLIC and to secure to all its citizens — Justice, Liberty, Equality — and to promote among them all Fraternity..."

What is it, and why was it made?

The Constitution of India is the supreme law of the land — the foundational document from which all legislative, executive, and judicial authority in India flows. It was drafted by the Constituent Assembly of India, which first met on December 9, 1946, and completed its work on November 26, 1949. It took 2 years, 11 months, and 17 days to draft.

The Constitution came into force on January 26, 1950 — a date chosen deliberately to mark the anniversary of the 1930 Purna Swaraj declaration. Dr. B.R. Ambedkar served as Chairman of the Drafting Committee, and the document drew from constitutions of the USA, UK, Ireland, Canada, Australia, Germany, and the USSR.

Scope and structure

Originally consisting of 395 Articles, 22 Parts, and 8 Schedules, the Constitution has since been amended 106 times (as of 2024). It establishes India as a sovereign, socialist, secular, democratic republic with a parliamentary system of government. The Fundamental Rights (Part III), Directive Principles (Part IV), and Fundamental Duties (Part IVA) form its philosophical core.

Why it matters for law students

Every area of law in India — criminal, civil, administrative, environmental, labour — derives its legitimacy from the Constitution. Articles 12–35 (Fundamental Rights) provide the basis for most PILs and writ petitions. Articles 32 and 226 are the two most litigated provisions in constitutional practice.

Key provisions at a glance

Article 12–35 — Fundamental Rights

Part III

Six fundamental rights: equality (14–18), freedom (19–22), against exploitation (23–24), religion (25–28), culture and education (29–30), and constitutional remedies (32). Article 32 is itself a fundamental right — the right to move the Supreme Court.

Article 36–51 — Directive Principles of State Policy

Part IV

Non-justiciable guidelines for state governance. Includes equal pay (Article 39(d)), free legal aid (39A), uniform civil code (44), living wage (43), and protection of environment (48A). The relationship between FRs and DPSPs was settled in Kesavananda Bharati (1973) and Minerva Mills (1980).

Articles 52–151 — The Union Government

Parts V & VI

Establishes the President, Vice-President, Council of Ministers, Parliament (Lok Sabha + Rajya Sabha), and the Supreme Court. Article 74(1) — the Council of Ministers shall aid and advise the President — is the foundation of parliamentary democracy.

Articles 352–360 — Emergency Provisions

Part XVIII

Three types: National Emergency (352), President's Rule (356), and Financial Emergency (360). The 44th Amendment (1978) made Article 352 proclamation subject to cabinet approval and parliamentary ratification within one month.

Landmark cases

Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)

AIR 1973 SC 1461 · 13-Judge Bench

Established the Basic Structure doctrine — Parliament cannot amend the Constitution so as to destroy its essential features. The basic structure includes supremacy of the Constitution, republican and democratic form of government, separation of powers, federalism, and judicial review.

Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978)

AIR 1978 SC 597

Expanded Article 21's "procedure established by law" to mean "just, fair, and reasonable procedure," incorporating the American concept of due process. This single judgment transformed Article 21 into the most expansive right in the Constitution.

S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994)

AIR 1994 SC 1918 · 9-Judge Bench

Subjected President's Rule under Article 356 to judicial review. Held that secularism is a basic feature of the Constitution and that the floor of the state legislature is the only proper forum to test a government's majority.

Justice K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2017)

(2017) 10 SCC 1 · 9-Judge Bench

Unanimously held that privacy is a fundamental right under Article 21. Overruled M.P. Sharma (1954) and Kharak Singh (1963). The three-fold test (legality, legitimate aim, proportionality) now governs all State interference with privacy.
